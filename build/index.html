The USGS Flood Event Viewer (FEV) is the public data discovery component of the Short-Term Network (STN) database. Data viewable and downloadable from this page are from the STN database.

This application relies on the STN Web Services to retrieve and display STN data. Application developers and general data seekers are encouraged to use the web services data API to retrieve STN data. Visit the STN Web Services Documentation Page for more information, including full documentation of available endpoints and examples. See the STN Data Dictionary for field names and definitions.

This application integrates with the USGS National Water Information System (NWIS) database for display of time-series water data. As a user of FEV you will encounter links to NWIS pages and data sources, which are maintained separately from STN and FEV.

Frequently Asked Questions


1. What is the FEV?
The Flood Event Viewer or FEV was created by the USGS to provide public access to coordinated, short snippets (lengths) of coastal and riverine water-level and highwater mark records corresponding to major storms or other short-term events. Long-term records for permanent USGS streamgages are available at the USGS National Water Information System (NWIS).
2. What is total water level (unfiltered water level)?
The total water level or unfiltered water level is the water surface measured and recorded by the sensor. During a coastal storm event, the total water level is defined as the combination of the astronomical tide, storm surge, and waves.
3. What is storm tide (low-pass filtered water level)?
The storm tide is defined as the water level caused by the combination of the astronomical tide and storm surge.
4. How does the USGS filter storm tide data?
The USGS uses the Butterworth Filter (low-pass filter) to attenuate high frequencies in order to remove the components of wave setup and runup to estimate the storm tide at the site. To learn more about the Butterworth Filter utilized by the USGS, please visit WaveLab.

The smoothness of the low-pass filtered line will depend on the sensor type (water level or wave sensor), on the location of where the sensor was deployed, the intensity of the storm, filter type, and filter window.

Listed below is the history of the use of the WaveLab Butterworth Filter utilized by the USGS:
- From 2005 - 2015, the USGS did not apply a low-pass filter to remove the components of wave setup and runup.
- From 2015-2020, the USGS used the Butterworth Filter with various filtering windows.
- Starting in 2021, with Hurricane Henri, the USGS standardized the filtering window to 6-minutes.
5. Who do I reach out to for questions about the data?
You can submit any data questions via our Answers form on the USGS website: https://answers.usgs.gov/.

For the 'Topic' field please select 'Water'.
6. How should I referencee your data in my publication?
Please reference the USGS Flood Event Viewer in all publications that use data from this system.

Template citation:
U.S. Geological Survey, [year], Short-Term Network Data Portal, accessed on [full-date], at http://water.usgs.gov/floods/FEV/.
